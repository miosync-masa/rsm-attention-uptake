# Discussion §X — The Tourist Phenomenon

**Working title:** "Beyond Selection Pressure: Attention as a Stand-Alone Communicative Layer"

**Status:** Draft v1 for incorporation into the IMT Attention Bias paper Discussion section, following the main empirical results and preceding limitations.

## §X.1 An everyday observation that troubles the standard model

The framework developed in this paper has thus far treated attention bias as a *selection pressure* on the input — a filter through which raw caregiver speech becomes learnable intake for grammar acquisition. This formulation, inherited from Lidz and Gagliardi (2015) and extended here with the five-dimensional salience model, presupposes that grammar is the *target* of attentional filtering: attention orients the learner toward the cues that will eventually be assembled into a grammatical system.

A simple observation, however, suggests this presupposition is too narrow.

Consider an adult monolingual Japanese speaker who travels to Hawaiʻi without any English instruction. Such a traveler routinely accomplishes the practical tasks of tourism — ordering food, finding restrooms, asking directions, expressing fatigue, negotiating prices, conveying preferences — using a combination of (i) isolated content words, (ii) prosodic contour, (iii) gesture and gaze, and (iv) a small set of frozen formulaic expressions ("thank you," "where," "how much"). At no point does this traveler deploy English grammar in any meaningful sense: there is no productive use of word order, no verbal morphology, no functional category distinction. And yet, communication succeeds — not merely in degraded form, but at a level comparable to the communicative repertoire of a thirty-month-old child engaged in the two-word stage of L1 acquisition.

This observation, which we will call the **Tourist Phenomenon**, is not theoretically novel; it has antecedents in the literatures on pidgin languages, basic variety in adult L2 acquisition, and gestural-prosodic communication. What is rarely articulated, however, is its implication for the model of attention developed in this paper: **the attentional system that filters input does not require the existence of a downstream grammar to be communicatively functional**.

## §X.2 Reframing the claim

The findings reported in §§3–6 — that utterance-edge cues exhibit the highest Attention Index across all seven languages examined, that cue Reliability dissociates into three non-redundant dimensions (R_gra, R_position, R_form), and that languages distribute grammatical information across typologically distinct carrier profiles — were originally framed as evidence for attention's role *in service of* grammar acquisition. The Tourist Phenomenon invites a stronger and more parsimonious reading: attention does not serve grammar. Attention constitutes a **communicative layer in its own right**, and grammar is built on top of it as a subsequent developmental and evolutionary stratum.

Under this revised reading, the cross-linguistic invariance of edge salience is not a "scaffold for grammar" but rather a property of the pre-grammatical communicative substrate itself. Caregivers across typologically distinct languages place high-salience material at utterance edges because **edges are where pre-grammatical communication lives**. The grammatical content that subsequently colonizes those positions — sentence-final particles in Japanese, modal auxiliaries in English, aspect markers in Mandarin — inherits the attentional salience of the underlying communicative slot, not the other way around.

This reframing makes three empirical predictions that the present dataset is consistent with but cannot definitively adjudicate, and which we offer for future work:

1. **Cross-linguistic AI invariance should hold even for non-grammatical material.** Discourse markers, interjections, address terms, and prosodic elongation should exhibit AI values comparable to or exceeding those of grammatical cues at the same position. Our data offer preliminary support: Japanese `elong_long` (prosodic ー) reached AI = 0.604, comparable to grammaticalized sentence-final particles (`particle_yo` AI = 0.695, `particle_ne` AI = 0.664). The boundary between "attention-attracting material that happens to be grammatical" and "attention-attracting material that is purely communicative" is itself blurred at the data level.

2. **Adult L2 learners should show preserved sensitivity to high-AI positions even without acquiring the grammar that occupies those positions.** The Tourist Phenomenon predicts that the attentional layer remains operative independent of grammatical proficiency: an adult Japanese speaker in Hawaiʻi does not need to know that English `is` is a copula to recognize that English utterance-initial position carries informational weight. This prediction is, in principle, testable through L2 attention tracking paradigms.

3. **Pre-grammatical communicative competence should be measurable as a stand-alone variable.** The traveler's success in Hawaiʻi cannot be reduced to "incomplete grammar"; it is the deployment of a distinct competence that does not require grammatical representation. A research program organized around this competence — distinct from both interlanguage grammar studies and pidgin/creole genesis studies — would treat pre-grammatical communication not as a developmental waypoint but as a persistent human capacity.

## §X.3 Relation to existing frameworks

The Tourist Phenomenon is consistent with, but conceptually distinct from, several established positions in linguistics and developmental psychology.

**Pidgin and Basic Variety research** (Klein and Perdue 1997; Bickerton 1981) has long documented that adult L2 learners without instruction converge on a strikingly similar minimal system: lexical items, basic word order tendencies, and prosodic structuring, with minimal functional morphology. The Tourist Phenomenon extends this observation in suggesting that the *attentional substrate* underlying this minimal system is itself a discoverable cross-linguistic invariant — not merely a default fallback when grammar is absent, but a functional layer on which grammar is optionally superimposed.

**Two-word stage research in L1 acquisition** (Braine 1963; Tomasello 2003) has similarly documented that prelinguistic and early-linguistic communication accomplishes a wide range of communicative functions without grammatical apparatus. What the present analysis adds is the proposal that the *attentional configuration* underlying two-word communication is the same configuration that supports adult tourist communication, and the same configuration that the five-dimensional Attention Index model measures across our seven languages. The continuity proposal makes the developmental claim non-trivial: it asserts that early L1 and late L2 success in pre-grammatical communication draw on a shared computational substrate, not on independent fallback mechanisms.

**Gesture-speech integration research** (Goldin-Meadow 2003; McNeill 1992) has demonstrated that gestural communication is not auxiliary to language but co-constitutive of communicative competence. The Tourist Phenomenon's reliance on gesture is, on this view, not a deficiency relative to grammatical communication but a feature of the pre-grammatical communicative layer itself.

What unifies these literatures and the present proposal is the recognition that grammar is one — but not the only — way that human attentional and communicative capacities can be deployed. The pre-grammatical layer is **what remains when grammar is absent**, and the empirical question of interest is what that layer *is*, not merely what it *lacks*.

## §X.4 Consequences for the IMT framework

The Invisible Mediator Theorem (IMT) holds that wherever a theoretical equation A = B is asserted, a hidden mediator M renders the equation conditional: A = B | (M ≈ constant). In the language acquisition literature, the conventional equation has been:

> Attention bias = Grammar-relevant input filter

The Tourist Phenomenon identifies a mediator that has been silently held constant throughout this literature: **the assumption that the learner is acquiring grammar**. Under this assumption, attention's role appears to be filtering for grammar. Once the assumption is relaxed — once we consider attentional systems operating in adults who are *not* acquiring grammar — the equation reveals its conditional form:

> Attention bias = Grammar-relevant input filter **| (Learner is grammar-acquiring)**

When the learner is not grammar-acquiring (the Hawaiʻi traveler, the speaker producing minimal-system communication), attention bias does not vanish. It continues to operate, but its function is no longer grammatical filtering. It is **direct communicative orientation**: marking what is informationally salient *for the immediate communicative exchange*, independent of any downstream grammatical computation.

This is the strongest form of the present paper's contribution. The five-dimensional Attention Index model, originally developed to characterize the pre-grammatical filter for L1 acquisition, generalizes to a broader claim: it characterizes the **pre-grammatical communicative layer itself**, of which L1 grammar acquisition is one (developmentally privileged) use case among several.

## §X.5 What this does not entail

We are careful not to overclaim. The Tourist Phenomenon does not entail that grammar is unnecessary or epiphenomenal; the communicative range of pre-grammatical exchange is bounded in ways that grammar manifestly extends. A traveler cannot, by lexico-prosodic-gestural means alone, narrate a counterfactual, embed a relative clause, or distinguish "the man saw the woman" from "the woman saw the man" without contextual support. Grammar dramatically expands communicative range, and L1 acquisition is properly described as the construction of this extended capacity.

What the Tourist Phenomenon does entail is that **the substrate on which grammar is built is itself communicatively functional**, and that the attentional mechanisms measured in this paper are properties of this substrate, not properties of grammar's acquisition trajectory specifically. The two claims — (i) attention enables grammar acquisition, and (ii) attention is constitutive of a pre-grammatical communicative layer in its own right — are not in tension. The second claim is the more general; the first is its specialization to the case of developing learners.

## §X.6 Future directions

The Tourist Phenomenon opens at least three lines of empirical inquiry that the present typological corpus analysis can inform but cannot resolve:

First, **comparative measurement of the attentional layer across naïve adult L2 contexts and L1 two-word stage children**. If the layer is a shared substrate, attention indices computed on tourist-directed simplified input and on caregiver speech to two-year-olds should converge on similar profiles for the universal dimensions (acoustic edge, position, repetition) while diverging on the language-specific form-based dimensions.

Second, **interventional studies in which adult L2 learners are exposed to grammatical and non-grammatical material with matched attentional profiles**, testing whether uptake into productive use is genuinely grammar-driven or whether it follows the pre-grammatical attentional layer regardless of grammatical status.

Third, **diachronic investigation of how pidgin-to-creole grammaticalization patterns relate to the high-AI positions identified in our typological survey**. If pre-grammatical communication preferentially occupies edge positions, then the morphemes that grammaticalize across the pidgin-creole transition should disproportionately come from edge-occupying material in the source pidgin. This is, to our knowledge, testable but untested.

We leave these questions for future work, and offer the Tourist Phenomenon here as an invitation to read the present results not as a study of how grammar is learned, but as a first quantitative characterization of the **pre-grammatical communicative layer that grammar inhabits**.
